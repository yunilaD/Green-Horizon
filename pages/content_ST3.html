Our Living Planet: An Overview

Terrestrial ecosystems are the foundation of life on Earth, supporting an incredible diversity of plants, animals, and microorganisms that together form the web of life. From dense rainforests to vast grasslands, from towering mountains to arid deserts, land ecosystems provide essential services that sustain all life, including humans.

Earth's terrestrial ecosystems are incredibly diverse, each playing a vital role in planetary health

These ecosystems regulate our climate, purify our water, produce oxygen, prevent soil erosion, and provide food, medicine, and materials that billions of people depend on daily. Yet despite their importance, human activities continue to degrade these vital systems at an alarming rate.

Current Threats to Life on Land

Our terrestrial ecosystems face unprecedented pressures from human activities. Understanding these threats is the first step toward addressing them effectively and creating a more sustainable relationship with our planet.

Habitat Fragmentation and Loss

The conversion of natural landscapes for agriculture, urban development, and infrastructure is the leading cause of habitat loss worldwide. When habitats are fragmented, species become isolated in small patches, reducing genetic diversity and increasing vulnerability to extinction.

Climate Change Impacts

Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and more frequent extreme weather events are altering ecosystems faster than many species can adapt. Climate change is shifting species ranges, disrupting seasonal cycles, and exacerbating other threats like wildfires and invasive species.

Climate change is transforming landscapes and threatening biodiversity worldwide

Pollution and Contamination

Chemical pollutants, plastics, and excess nutrients from agricultural runoff degrade soil and water quality, harming plants and animals. Even remote ecosystems now show evidence of human-produced contaminants, demonstrating the global reach of pollution.

Overexploitation of Resources

Unsustainable harvesting of timber, wildlife, and other natural resources depletes populations and disrupts ecosystem functions. The illegal wildlife trade alone threatens thousands of species and generates billions in illicit revenue annually.

Critical Ecosystems: Hotspots of Biodiversity

While all ecosystems contribute to Earth's biodiversity, some regions harbor exceptional concentrations of unique species and face severe threats. These biodiversity hotspots require urgent conservation attention.

Tropical Rainforests

Covering just 6% of Earth's land surface, tropical rainforests host over 50% of the world's plant and animal species. These complex ecosystems store vast amounts of carbon, regulate regional rainfall patterns, and provide livelihoods for millions of people.

Tropical rainforests are among the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth

Grasslands and Savannas

These vast ecosystems support incredible diversity of large mammals, birds, and specialized plants adapted to seasonal rainfall and periodic fires. Grasslands also store substantial carbon in their extensive root systems and soils.

Mediterranean Ecosystems

Found on five continents, Mediterranean-type ecosystems are characterized by hot, dry summers and mild, wet winters. Despite covering just 2% of Earth's land area, they contain nearly 20% of the world's plant species, many found nowhere else.

Mountain Ecosystems

Mountains create diverse microclimates that support specialized species adapted to different elevations. These "water towers" provide freshwater to billions of people but are highly vulnerable to climate change impacts.

Innovative Solutions for Conservation

Protecting life on land requires innovative approaches that balance human needs with ecosystem health. Fortunately, many promising solutions are already being implemented around the world.

Nature-Based Solutions

Nature-based solutions harness the power of healthy ecosystems to address societal challenges. Examples include restoring wetlands for flood control, reforesting watersheds to secure water supplies, and protecting coastal forests to buffer against storms.

Community-led reforestation projects restore ecosystems while providing sustainable livelihoods

Sustainable Agriculture

Agro-ecological approaches like agroforestry, permaculture, and regenerative farming can produce food while maintaining biodiversity, building soil health, and sequestering carbon. These methods often draw on indigenous knowledge combined with modern science.

Technology for Conservation

Emerging technologies are revolutionizing conservation efforts. Satellite monitoring tracks deforestation in real-time, DNA barcoding helps identify wildlife products in illegal trade, and environmental DNA (eDNA) allows scientists to detect species from water or soil samples.

Indigenous Stewardship

Indigenous peoples manage or have tenure rights to at least 38% of the world's remaining intact forests. Research consistently shows that indigenous-managed lands maintain higher biodiversity levels than conventional protected areas, highlighting the importance of supporting indigenous land rights and traditional knowledge systems.

Global Initiatives Making a Difference

Around the world, ambitious initiatives are working to protect and restore terrestrial ecosystems. These collaborative efforts demonstrate what's possible when governments, organizations, and communities work together toward common goals.

Bonn Challenge

The Bonn Challenge aims to restore 350 million hectares of degraded and deforested landscapes by 2030. So far, over 60 countries, subnational governments, and private organizations have committed to restoring 210 million hectares.

Landscape restoration can transform degraded areas into productive, biodiverse ecosystems

30x30 Initiative

This global target aims to protect 30% of Earth's land and ocean by 2030. Achieving this goal would significantly increase habitat for threatened species, secure ecosystem services, and help mitigate climate change through natural carbon storage.

UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration

Running from 2021 to 2030, this UN initiative aims to prevent, halt, and reverse ecosystem degradation worldwide. It provides a framework for coordinating restoration efforts across different ecosystems and scales, from local community projects to national programs.

Take Action: Be Part of the Solution

Everyone has a role to play in protecting life on land. Your actions, choices, and voice can make a meaningful difference in conservation efforts. Here are some ways you can contribute to a healthier planet:

Make Sustainable Choices

Support companies with verified sustainable practices. Look for certifications like FSC for wood products, Rainforest Alliance for crops, and organic for reduced pesticide use.

Create Wildlife Habitat

Transform your yard or community space into habitat by planting native species, reducing chemical use, and providing water sources for wildlife.

Participate in Citizen Science

Join projects like iNaturalist or eBird to help scientists track biodiversity. Your observations contribute to our understanding of species distributions and population trends.

Advocate for Nature

Support policies that protect natural habitats and promote sustainable land management. Contact elected officials, join conservation organizations, and amplify indigenous voices in land stewardship.

Remember that individual actions, when multiplied across communities and countries, create powerful momentum for change. By connecting with nature, sharing your knowledge, and taking consistent action, you become part of the global movement to protect life on land for future generations.
